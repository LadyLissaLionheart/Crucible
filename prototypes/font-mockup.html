One font per job

02 — Display / Headings

Bebas Neue

Bebas Neue Chapter Titles & Display
The Core Mechanic
Why this font: Bebas Neue is a clean, geometric all-caps display face with uniform letterforms — every letter carries the same weight and presence. No letter dominates or looks different from the rest. The condensed proportions pack tightly into headings without wasting space. It signals "this is important" with clarity and precision, without the decorative baggage of serif display fonts. The generous letter-spacing keeps it breathable at all sizes.

03 — Body Text

Libre Baskerville

Libre Baskerville Body / Long-Form Reading
Every meaningful action in Crucible is resolved with a single roll of 4d12. The player counts successes against a Target Number, while the GM applies Risk as a penalty zone. No lookups, no conditional math — just the dice and a single number. This simplicity is intentional: the resolution mechanic should become invisible within a session, so players focus on the fiction, not the arithmetic.
Why this font: Libre Baskerville is a digital revival of the classic Baskerville typeface, optimized for screen reading with a tall x-height and open letterforms. It's the most readable free serif at body text sizes. The generous spacing between letters and lines means players can scan quickly during play. It has the gravitas of a traditional rulebook without the stuffiness of Times New Roman. Italics are clean and distinct — critical for calling out game terms and emphasis.

04 — UI / Data / Labels

Inter

Inter Tables, Stat Blocks, Labels, UI
Might 5

Why this font: Inter was built for computer interfaces at small sizes — exactly the problem tables and stat blocks have. Its tabular figures align numbers in columns perfectly. The tight letter spacing and large x-height mean you can pack data into dense tables without sacrificing readability. It's the most-used free sans-serif for a reason. When a player glances at a stat block mid-combat, Inter gets out of the way.

The Core Mechanic
Resolution
Every meaningful action in Crucible is resolved with a single roll of 4d12. The player counts successes against a Target Number (TN), while the GM applies Risk as a penalty zone. No lookups, no conditional math.
The Three-Zone Die
Condition Result Meaning
Die ≤ Risk −1 success The danger claimed this die
Risk < die < TN 0 Neutral
Die ≥ TN +1 success The character's skill prevailed
The Success Ladder
Net successes map to a three-tier ladder. This isn't just pass/fail — it gives the GM narrative texture for every outcome, from catastrophic failure to triumphant breakthrough.
Net Tier Meaning
−1 or less Misfortune Failure, and things get worse
0 Setback Failure, no additional harm
1+ Triumph Clean success, and beyond
06 — What We're Not Using

Rejected Alternatives

Other fonts that were considered and why they didn't make the cut.

EB Garamond — Beautiful, but too delicate at small sizes. The thin strokes blur on cheaper prints and low-res PDFs. Libre Baskerville is more robust.
Crimson Text — Great personality, but the x-height is smaller than Baskerville's. At 10–11pt body text, it feels slightly cramped. Libre Baskerville wins on pure readability.
Source Serif 4 — Excellent modern serif, but it feels clinical. Crucible needs warmth — a hint of the old-world without being dusty. Baskerville splits the difference.
Gentium Book Plus — Designed for books, gorgeous italics, but the character set is large and the file size reflects it. For a PDF/rulebook, Libre Baskerville is lighter and more universally available.
IM Fell English — Gorgeous old-print aesthetic, but too rough for body text. The irregular edges that look charming at 24pt become noise at 11pt. Reserved for decorative moments only.
